HOMEWORK SHEET
============================================================

Friction: [Specific recurring problem or instability]

Rule: [Observed structural rule underlying this friction]

Belief: [Core distortion driving the friction]

Decision: [Typical decision error resulting from the belief]

Outcome: [Immediate consequence]

Reinforcement: [How the outcome strengthens the original belief]

Alignment: [Corrected knowledge, decision, and action]

============================================================
Let’s make it precise so it doesn’t become vague thinking.

Before you outline a 5–6k book, you must manually clarify these seven elements yourself:

Friction
Rule
Belief
Decision
Outcome
Reinforcement
Alignment

If you cannot articulate them clearly in plain language, the book will feel blurry.

Now let me sharpen what each one must contain so your preparation is rigorous.

---

# 1️⃣ Friction

Not a theme.
Not a category.
Not “low confidence.”

It must be:
A repeatable, observable instability.

Good:
“Losing confidence after public criticism.”

Weak:
“Confidence issues.”

Test:
Can you describe one vivid scene where this friction appears?
If yes → strong.
If no → too abstract.

---

# 2️⃣ Rule

Not advice.
Not morality.
Not philosophy.

It must be:
An observed recurring pattern.

Good:
“Skill improves through corrective feedback.”

Weak:
“You should listen to others.”

Test:
Can this rule operate even if someone doesn’t believe in it?
If yes → structural.
If no → opinion.

---

# 3️⃣ Belief

This is the distortion.
It must feel psychologically reasonable.

Good:
“If I’m criticized, it means I’m incompetent.”

Weak:
“People think negatively.”

Test:
Can you explain why someone would logically hold this belief?
If yes → usable.
If not → too shallow.

---

# 4️⃣ Decision

This is behavioral.
It must be specific.

Good:
“Avoid public speaking after criticism.”

Weak:
“React badly.”

Test:
Can you visualize the action clearly?

---

# 5️⃣ Outcome

Immediate consequence.
Not long-term destiny.

Good:
“Reduced exposure to feedback.”

Weak:
“Life gets worse.”

Keep it concrete.

---

# 6️⃣ Reinforcement

This is critical.
Show how the outcome strengthens the belief.

Example:
Avoid speaking
→ No new feedback
→ Skill stagnates
→ Next attempt feels worse
→ “See? I’m not good at this.”

If you cannot map reinforcement clearly,
the book will lack psychological depth.

---

# 7️⃣ Alignment

This must contain three layers:
Corrected knowledge
Corrected decision
Corrected action

If alignment is vague (“think positively”), it will weaken your framework.

It must be structural:
New understanding
→ New decision
→ New behavior
→ New outcome
→ New reinforcement

That builds bounce-back confidence.

---

# Your Real Discipline Rule

If you can write these seven in one page clearly,
you are ready to outline.

If you struggle to define even one,
do not outline yet.
Refine first.

---

# A Critical Insight

Most weak self-help books fail because:
They start with solutions.
You are starting with structure.
That is stronger.
============================================================

============================================================
Bottom-up is the right instinct for your 5–6k sharp books.

Because you chose:
One specific friction (narrow and sharp)

So your thinking should flow like this:
Friction → Belief → Decision → Outcome → Reinforcement → Rule → Alignment

Notice something important:
The Rule does not come first in bottom-up thinking.
It emerges from analysis.
That keeps you honest.

---

# The Correct Bottom-Up Workflow

Let’s make this practical.
Before outlining a book, sit with one friction and do this exercise.

---

## Step 1 — Describe the Friction Like a Scene

Write one paragraph describing:
• A concrete situation
• What the person feels
• What they think
• What they do

No theory.
Only observation.
If you can’t write a vivid scene, the friction is too abstract.

---

## Step 2 — Extract the Belief

Ask:
“What must this person believe for this reaction to make sense?”

Belief must be:
• Internally logical
• Emotionally protective
• Repeated across incidents

If the belief sounds stupid, you haven’t found the real one yet.

---

## Step 3 — Identify the Decision Error

Ask:
“What action naturally follows from this belief?”

Make it behavioral.
Avoid vague labels like “reacts poorly.”
Make it visible.

---

## Step 4 — Map the Immediate Outcome

Ask:
“What immediately happens because of that decision?”

Not destiny.
Not life collapse.
Just immediate consequence.

---

## Step 5 — Reveal the Reinforcement

This is the turning point.

Ask:
“How does that outcome confirm the original belief?”

If you cannot show reinforcement clearly,
the friction will feel random instead of structural.

---

## Step 6 — Only Now Extract the Rule

Now ask:
“What structural pattern is being violated here?”

The rule should explain:
Why the outcome is predictable.

If your rule feels moral or preachy,
refine it.

It must feel like:
Observed pattern.
Not advice.

---

## Step 7 — Design Alignment

Alignment must reverse the loop:
Corrected knowledge
→ New decision
→ New outcome
→ New reinforcement

If alignment feels motivational,
it is weak.

If alignment feels structural,
it is strong.

---

# Why Bottom-Up Is Powerful

Because:
You are not forcing a rule onto reality.
You are discovering it from reality.
That keeps your framework grounded.

---

# One Important Warning

Bottom-up can produce shallow rules if you rush.
If the rule feels obvious or generic,
go deeper.

Ask:
“What deeper structural pattern explains this?”
